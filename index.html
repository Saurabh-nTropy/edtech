Rocketry & Propulsion

Course Details

About this Course

This course provides a comprehensive overview of the fundamental principles of rocketry and propulsion systems. We will journey from the historical development of rockets to the cutting-edge technologies that could one day take us to other stars. Students will explore the physics of rocket flight, the chemistry of propellants, and the engineering challenges of building reliable launch vehicles.

What You'll Learn

  • Newton's Laws of Motion as they apply to rockets.
  • The Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation and its implications.
  • Differences between solid, liquid, and hybrid rocket engines.
  • Fundamentals of orbital mechanics.
  • Advanced propulsion concepts like ion drives and solar sails.

Intro to Astrophysics

Course Details

About this Course

Journey through the cosmos and uncover the secrets of the stars, galaxies, and the very fabric of spacetime. This course introduces the core concepts of astrophysics, from the lifecycle of stars to the enigmatic nature of dark matter and dark energy. No advanced math is required to start—just a universe of curiosity.

What You'll Learn

  • The birth, life, and death of stars.
  • The different types of galaxies and our place in the Milky Way.
  • The basics of black holes and Einstein's theory of relativity.
  • How astronomers measure vast cosmic distances.
  • The Big Bang theory and the expansion of the universe.

Satellite Technology

Course Details

About this Course

Discover the unseen infrastructure that powers our modern world. This course explores the design, launch, and operation of artificial satellites. We'll cover everything from the CubeSats built by students to the massive constellations that provide global internet and the scientific instruments that monitor our planet's health.

What You'll Learn

  • The main components of a satellite: bus, payload, and power systems.
  • Different types of orbits (LEO, MEO, GEO) and their uses.
  • How GPS and global navigation systems work.
  • The role of satellites in Earth observation and climate science.
  • The challenges of space debris and satellite maintenance.

Colonizing Mars

Course Details

About this Course

Becoming a multi-planetary species is one of humanity's most ambitious goals. This course tackles the immense scientific and engineering challenges of establishing a permanent, self-sustaining human presence on Mars. We will analyze everything from the journey to Mars to building habitats, producing food, and creating a new society on another world.

What You'll Learn

  • The environmental conditions on Mars and their dangers.
  • Strategies for radiation shielding and life support systems.
  • In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU): living off the land.
  • The psychological and social challenges of long-duration space missions.
  • Terraforming: the long-term vision of making Mars Earth-like.

Webb Telescope Unveils a Galaxy in its Infancy

Article from the Cosmos

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has once again peered back in time to the dawn of the universe. In a stunning new deep field image, astronomers have identified what they believe to be one of the earliest galaxies ever observed, existing just 300 million years after the Big Bang. Its light, stretched by the expansion of the universe for over 13.5 billion years, reveals a compact but rapidly forming collection of stars. This discovery challenges existing models of early galaxy formation and provides a crucial data point in our understanding of cosmic evolution.

Artemis Program: What's Next for Lunar Exploration?

Article from the Cosmos

With the successful uncrewed flight of Artemis I, NASA is marching steadily towards landing the first woman and first person of color on the Moon. The next major milestone, Artemis II, will send a crewed Orion capsule on a journey around the Moon, paving the way for the Artemis III landing mission. The program's ultimate goal is to establish a sustainable human presence on the lunar surface, including a base camp and the Gateway orbital outpost. These efforts will serve as a vital proving ground for the technologies and procedures needed for the even greater challenge: sending humans to Mars.

Is Fusion Propulsion the Key to Interstellar Travel?

Article from the Cosmos

Chemical rockets, while powerful, are fundamentally too slow for practical travel between stars. The next great leap in propulsion may come from harnessing the power of the stars themselves: nuclear fusion. A fusion drive would fuse light atoms like deuterium and helium-3 to release enormous amounts of energy, creating a high-velocity exhaust. While the engineering challenges are immense—requiring contained temperatures hotter than the sun's core—the payoff would be revolutionary. A fusion-powered spacecraft could potentially reach Mars in a matter of weeks and could make journeys to the nearest stars possible within a human lifetime.
